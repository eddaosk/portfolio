SaaS · Vending Machine · Sustainability

JuicOrganic

Smart Vending Machine Kiosk UI

UX RESEARCH · INTERACTION DESIGN · FRONT-END DEVELOPMENT

JuicOrganic had strong products and a clear ecological profile — but no way to reach consumers directly. The brief was open: get them in front of everyday people. We chose the vending machine direction, then had to solve the real challenge — designing a kiosk that works in under five minutes, in a busy transit location, for someone who's never used it before.

The Challenge

JuicOrganic was a B2B juice brand ready to go direct-to-consumer. Once we identified commuters and on-the-go buyers as the primary target group, the UX problem became clear: the kiosk had to work in a noisy transit environment, in under five minutes, for a first-time user with no patience for friction. Every extra step was a lost sale.

What We Did

  • Desk research into vending machine culture globally — especially Japan's 24/7 model — and field research at Copenhagen Central Station, observing users at touchscreen kiosks in fast food restaurants
  • Two in-depth interviews with a dancer (29) and a project manager (35) — both on-the-go with sustainability values — uncovering what drives purchase decisions, contactless payment expectations, and attitudes toward refillable bottles
  • Two user personas and journey maps covering a brand-loyal primary segment and a commuter/tourist secondary segment
  • Built the concept around three pillars: a touchscreen kiosk UI, a SaaS management layer for JuicOrganic to control inventory and pricing in real time, and a barcode-based reusable bottle reward system
  • Tested the moodboard (think-aloud), the logo (5 participants — 4/5 chose the final design), and ran an A/B test comparing illustrated vs. product photo layouts — 6/8 users chose photos for the ordering screen, so we used illustrations only for the custom refill flow
  • Three rounds of usability testing with real participants — identified missing price information and an unclear bottle-buying flow, and iterated to fix both before final delivery
  • Built a design system in Figma using variables and swap instance for efficient component management across the full kiosk UI
  • Coded the front-end in HTML, SCSS, JavaScript, and Bootstrap — product data loaded from object arrays, cart stored in LocalStorage, dynamic badge on the cart icon
  • Produced a campaign video in After Effects that doubled as the kiosk's idle screensaver, plus a five-week social media content plan and SEO strategy for the B2C launch

The Outcome

We delivered a working coded prototype, a complete Figma design system, and a full communication strategy. The biggest UX gains came from cutting steps, not adding features — after iteration, users could complete a purchase in under two minutes. The key insight: in a vending machine context, clarity always beats creativity. The project received top marks (12).

EdTech · Neuro-Inclusive · Motion Design

GradeAid — The Deep Blue

Neuro-Inclusive Gamified Learning Concept

GAMIFICATION DESIGN · UX RESEARCH · MOTION DESIGN

GradeAid had working AI technology for teaching neurodivergent children — but needed a visual identity, characters, and a way to make the experience feel safe. When we researched the target group, we found that standard EdTech design could actively overstimulate the very users it was meant to support. Traditional gamification creates a dopamine chase. Bright colours and busy interfaces trigger sensory overload. We had to rethink the whole thing from the ground up.

The Challenge

The client had a working AI teaching platform and needed a visual identity, a logo, and animated characters to guide children through the app. The target group was neurodivergent children and youth — primarily those with ADHD, autism, and sensory processing disorders — many experiencing school refusal. Our research revealed that existing EdTech choices assumed a neurotypical learner. High-contrast colours, competitive reward structures, and busy interfaces were creating the very overstimulation they were meant to help with.

What We Did

  • Desk research into neuro-inclusive design, Blue Mind theory, WCAG colour science, and why traditional gamification (dopamine-chase reward loops) creates stress rather than calm for neurodivergent learners
  • Expert interview with a special needs teacher on colour palettes, motivation systems, and gamification design — validating our direction on reward structure and age-adaptive tone
  • Reframed the entire experience around Blue Mind theory: water as a neurological tool, not just a visual theme — calming blue/green palettes with shorter wavelength spectrums that require less metabolic energy in the visual cortex
  • Tuned the client's original colour palette for WCAG accessibility, reducing visual stress without losing brand identity
  • Designed three non-human characters — Lix, Vitu, and Kiku — with tintable skins and age-adaptive evolution, avoiding human avatars to reduce social pressure and rejection-sensitive dysphoria (RSD)
  • Built an age-adaptive design system: Lavvande zone (grades 1–4) uses soft, playful interfaces and warm tones; Abyssal Zone (grades 9–10) switches to dark mode with deep navy and sci-fi language
  • Designed a gamification system where progress is shown as a growing coral reef — quick rewards called Muslingeskaller for younger children evolve into Bio-Lumens for teenagers, balancing immediate feedback with long-term satisfaction
  • Added a Focus Mode with brown noise and wave sounds to support sensory regulation during learning
  • Produced character animations in After Effects using slow, fluid movement to avoid vestibular overstimulation
  • Built a user journey map and prototype scenario for Holger, 8 years old, 2nd grade, ADHD — tested the prototype with a real child and incorporated feedback on text density and colour softness
  • Completed a Value Proposition Canvas and Business Model Canvas to frame the product strategy

The Outcome

What began as a visual identity and character brief became a full product reframe. Every design decision — colour, motion, typography, reward structure — was grounded in neurological research rather than aesthetics. The child test confirmed the calmer direction worked. The expert validated the gamification logic. The key learning: for neurodivergent users, design has to earn its choices. The project received top marks (12).

FinTech · UX Research · Information Architecture

Econiq

FinTech SaaS Prototype

UX LEAD · INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE · USER TESTING

A FinTech founder had a solid concept — an AI-powered forecasting tool for small business owners — but the prototype was hard to navigate. We led the UX redesign, rebuilt the information architecture, tightened the onboarding, and delivered a user-tested prototype that non-experts could complete without help.

The Challenge

The concept was strong, but the prototype (built with AI tools) lacked structure: no navigation, inconsistent layouts, and an onboarding flow with high drop-off risk. The core UX challenge was making financial forecasting and dashboard data approachable for small business owners without a finance background.

What We Did

  • Audited the original prototype and documented usability and IA issues
  • Rebuilt the information architecture and introduced a clear navigation model
  • Redesigned onboarding to reduce steps and increase clarity at each decision point
  • Built the redesigned flow in Figma
  • Validated with 5 think-aloud usability tests with members of a local startup community
  • Expert review with a financial instructor to validate domain-specific assumptions

The Outcome

We delivered a usable foundation for launch — a coherent product structure and a working prototype the founder accepted as the direction forward. In testing, users could complete the forecasting questionnaire and read the dashboard without assistance. A clear improvement over the original flow.

HOW WE WORK

Research-first. Every time.

Every design decision is backed by user testing, anthropological thinking, and logic — not just aesthetics. We challenge each other's assumptions, iterate quickly, and don't skip steps just to be done.

Step 01 Understand

We talk to you, your users, and anyone close to the problem. Before we design anything, we need to know what's actually going on.

Step 02 Define

We map out the problem, the user journey, and what a good solution looks like. This is where the direction gets set.

Step 03 Design & Test

From rough sketches to high-fidelity screens. We test with real users and refine until it works.

Step 04 Build & Deliver

A coded, working prototype you can actually click through — plus documentation and a clean handover.
