JuicOrganic

Smart Vending Machine Kiosk UI

UX Research · Interaction Design · Front-End Development

JuicOrganic is a Danish juice company operating B2B. We were challenged to take them direct-to-consumer — and instead of designing a typical landing page, we created a smart vending machine concept that encourages bottle reuse through a digital kiosk interface with a built-in reward system. I led the UX research and built the coded prototype.

View Case Study
The Challenge

The brief was open-ended: find a way to bring JuicOrganic to consumers. The real design challenge came after we landed on the vending machine concept — how do you design a kiosk interface for users who are in a hurry? We observed existing touch interfaces (McDonald’s ordering kiosks, coffee machines) and quickly realised our early wireframes had too many steps. The placement of the machines — transit hubs, busy locations — meant the interaction had to be fast and intuitive.

My Role & Process
  • Conducted 2 in-depth interviews using anthropological methods on sustainability attitudes and reusable bottle adoption
  • Translated insights into user journey maps, feature priorities, and user scenarios
  • Iterated wireframes heavily — stripping steps until the flow matched the speed users needed
  • Teammates took wireframes into high-fidelity Figma (visual design + illustrations)
  • Coded a complete user flow as a working front-end prototype (HTML, SCSS, JavaScript, Bootstrap), optimised for iPad Pro
  • Animated the idle-screen promotional video in After Effects, doubling as social media content
Outcome

We delivered a working coded prototype, a high-fidelity Figma design, and a promotional video — a full product concept, not just screens. The project received top marks (12). The part I’m most proud of is the concept itself: we could have played it safe with a simple website, but we thought bigger and backed it up with real research and a functional prototype.

Econiq

FinTech SaaS Prototype

UX Lead · Information Architecture · User Testing

A FinTech founder needed to turn a complex financial forecasting tool into something non-experts could actually use. His existing prototype — largely AI-generated — had no navigation, inconsistent layouts, and an information architecture that confused even financially literate users. We delivered a complete redesign: a cohesive brand identity and a tested Figma prototype built from the ground up.

View Case Study
The Challenge

The founder had a working concept but the prototype was built through AI tools without design expertise. The result: no navigation system, too many steps in the onboarding flow, a disjointed visual identity, and an information architecture that didn't hold up under scrutiny. The core challenge was making dense financial data — forecasting questionnaires, business dashboards — feel approachable for small business owners with no finance background.

My Role & Process

Two-person design team — I led UX, my partner handled visual identity and UI.

  • Audited the founder’s existing AI-generated prototype, documenting every usability issue
  • Restructured the information architecture — reducing steps and introducing clear navigation
  • Reorganised the onboarding wizard so users could complete it without getting lost
  • Built the redesigned prototype in Figma
  • Tested with 5 users (think-aloud sessions + interviews) from a local startup community
  • Ran expert reviews with a financial instructor to validate domain-specific flows
Outcome

The founder received two things he didn't have before: a professional brand identity and a usable prototype. He accepted the redesign as the foundation for his product launch. Testing confirmed that users could navigate the forecasting questionnaire and dashboard without assistance — a clear improvement over the original, where even expert reviewers struggled with the flow.

GradeAid

AI-Powered Learning Assistant

Gamification Design · UX Research · Motion Design

GradeAid is a DTU-backed edtech startup building an AI learning platform for children who need extra support. We were briefed to design a visual identity and create three animated characters. We went further — grounding the entire product in Blue Mind theory and neuro-inclusive design research, then reimagining it as a gamified underwater world built specifically for neurodivergent learners.

View Case Study
The Challenge

The client had working AI technology but needed a visual identity, logo, and at least three animated characters with tintable looks and accessories to guide children through the app. When we researched the target group — neurodivergent children experiencing school refusal — we found that the existing design choices (color palette, interaction patterns) risked overstimulating the very users they were meant to help. Most edtech assumes a motivated, neurotypical learner. We needed to design an experience grounded in how neurodivergent brains actually process visual information, motivation, and failure.

My Role & Process
  • Contributed to desk research on neuro-inclusive design and Blue Mind theory
  • Conducted an expert interview with a special educator on color palettes and gamification for neurodivergent children
  • Created a 5-phase user journey map from discovery through advocacy
  • Designed the full gamification system — coral reef metaphor, XP/currency loop, age-adaptive progression, and reward shop
  • Co-designed the game world characters (Lix, Vitu, Kiko) with tintable skins and age-adaptive visual evolution
  • Contributed to the visual identity alongside teammates
  • Produced character animations in After Effects — slow, fluid movements to avoid vestibular overstimulation
Outcome

The project received top marks (12). Instructors complimented the team’s collaboration — noting all three members were equally engaged across every aspect of the work. What started as a visual identity and character animation brief became a complete product reimagining. We challenged the client’s existing color choices with research-backed alternatives, introduced a gamification system they hadn’t asked for, and delivered a concept grounded in real testing with children and expert validation from a special educator.

Das Kleine Büro

Digital Agency Simulation

Project Management · Team Leadership · Client Coordination

A six-week simulation of a real digital agency: nine people, five client accounts, one remote team member in a different time zone. I managed the team's workflow, formed sub-teams based on individual skill profiles, and kept multiple client deliveries on track — while learning to step into a leadership role I didn't initially expect.

View Case Study
The Challenge

The scope was deliberately overwhelming — that was the point. Nine people with different skill sets, ambition levels, and working styles needed to deliver real work to real clients simultaneously. The challenge wasn't just producing good design work — it was building a team structure that could actually function under pressure, with clear communication channels and accountability.

My Role & Process
  • Surveyed the team on hard skills, soft skills, and working preferences to form sub-teams
  • Assigned client accounts — matching people to projects for maximum effectiveness
  • Introduced daily stand-ups for cross-project visibility and early bottleneck detection
  • Assigned single points of contact per client to keep communication focused
  • People management — encouraging ownership, stepping in during bottlenecks, maintaining quality consistency
  • Took direct ownership of one client case (Econiq) alongside coordination duties
Outcome

All client projects were delivered on deadline. When three agency teams had to choose one team to collaborate with, both local teams selected the international team — but the international team chose us, citing our inclusive team culture. Instructors highlighted our collaboration as a standout. The biggest personal takeaway: I went in calling myself "just a coordinator" and came out understanding that effective teams need someone willing to lead, not just organise.

Hoedown After Dark

E-commerce Brand

Brand Strategy · Visual Design · Project Coordination

A fashion brand blending Western aesthetics with underground techno culture and the queer community. We designed the brand, built a web shop, and sold real products — donating all revenue to LGBT Asylum. I led the brand strategy and project coordination while shaping the visual identity.

Read Case Study
The Challenge

The brief was to create a working e-commerce brand from scratch — product design, web shop, and actual sales. The real challenge was building a brand with a genuine identity, not just a student exercise. We leaned into the group’s shared connection to queer culture and the techno scene, which gave the brand an authenticity that a more generic concept wouldn’t have had.

My Role & Process
  • Led brand strategy — Business Model Canvas, SEO, and persuasive design principles
  • Applied CTA hierarchy and donation framing to drive conversions and charity contributions
  • Validated the concept using Lean Startup methods: MVP tested at a flea market in a techno club with queer roots
  • Used direct target-audience feedback to shape the final product line and brand direction
Outcome

We launched a functioning web shop, sold real T-shirts, tote bags, and stickers, and donated all revenue — nearly 1,000 DKK — to LGBT Asylum. We were the only group to donate proceeds and the top-selling group at the project showcase. The brand gained genuine traction on Instagram, including a DJ posting a photo wearing our T-shirt, which drove significant engagement. The project stands out in my portfolio for being the most creatively different — and for proving that leaning into a specific subculture creates a far stronger brand than trying to appeal to everyone.

Gestalt

Digital Magazine

Editorial Design · Photography · Content Creation

A feature article for a collaborative digital magazine, arguing that echo chambers aren’t just an online phenomenon — they’re built into our cultures, languages, and communities. I wrote the article, photographed all original assets, and designed the layout in InDesign using Gestalt principles and strict grid systems.

View Case Study
The Challenge

The theme was perception and reality. I needed to find an angle that went beyond the obvious “social media is bad” take, then execute every part — concept, research, writing, photography, and layout — as a solo project.

My Role & Process
  • Drew on anthropological background to frame echo chambers as everyday phenomena: culture as “the original algorithm”, language as a filter, communities as feedback loops
  • Interviewed friends to avoid writing from inside my own bubble — used their quotes throughout
  • Photographed real people to create a visual language reflecting the theme
  • Edited photography in Lightroom
  • Designed the full layout in InDesign using Gestalt principles and grid systems
Outcome

The article was published in the final collaborative magazine. One instructor noted the layout looked like it could belong in a professional publication. The project demonstrates visual thinking, grid-based design, and the ability to own an entire creative process from concept to finished product.
